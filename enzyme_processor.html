How to Use ALOE
Automated Ligand Optimisation Engine — Step-by-step guide
Step 1 of 7
Upload Your Structural Files
Start by uploading two files: your enzyme structure (.pdb) and your ligand structure (.sdf). Click each upload box to browse your filesystem and select the files.
Supported formats: Enzyme → .pdb only  |  Ligand → .sdf only. Download structures from RCSB PDB and PubChem respectively.
Step 2 of 7
Choose Scaffold Mode
Select how the ligand scaffold should be prepared. Use molecule as-is keeps the full ligand as the starting scaffold. BRICS fragmentation algorithmically breaks the molecule at retrosynthetically meaningful bonds and keeps the largest fragment as the scaffold, useful for complex multi-ring ligands.
Option A — As-is (recommended for small ligands)
Option B — BRICS (complex multi-ring ligands)
After selecting, the ligand is automatically processed and displayed in the 3D Protein-Ligand Explorer below. The Scaffold SMILES field will populate with the detected structure.
Step 3 of 7
The BRICS Scaffold Loads in the 3D Viewer
After uploading, the scaffold appears in the Protein-Ligand Explorer as an interactive 3D ball-and-stick model. When BRICS fragmentation is selected, the largest chemically meaningful fragment of your ligand is shown and the attachment-point carbon of the adjacent fragment is removed by the algorithm, so the BRICS scaffold may have one fewer carbon than the original molecule (this is expected). The Scaffold SMILES field below the viewer shows its exact structure.
Atom colours: Grey = Carbon  |  Red = Oxygen  |  Blue = Nitrogen. Carbons with existing substitutions are not selectable — they have no free hydrogen. Only CH, CH₂, CH₃ carbons can be substitution sites.
Step 4 of 7
Click a Carbon Atom to Create a Substitution Job
Click directly on a grey carbon atom to mark it as a substitution site. It highlights in red and a job card is instantly created below with the auto-generated SMILES showing [*] on that carbon. The SMILES is built from the BRICS scaffold or the full original molecule, as chosen. Up to 3 sites per run.
Reading the SMILES: CCCC([*])C=O — starting from the terminal CH₃, the chain goes C–C–C–C([*])–C=O. The [*] is on C-4, exactly where you clicked. This SMILES is what gets combined with each library fragment during the pipeline.
Step 5 of 7
Set Molecular Weight Range for Each Job
Each job card has a Molecular Weight Range selector. This controls which fragments from the library are tested at that substitution site. A tighter MW window means fewer, more targeted fragments; a wider window explores more chemical space.
Recommended: Start with 0–350 Da (Optimum) for the first run. Use Custom to enter a specific min/max range if you want to target a particular size class of fragments (e.g. only tiny groups like methyl/ethyl at 0–30 Da).
Step 6 of 7
Click Process and Watch the Pipeline
Click the Process button to launch the pipeline. The progress bar and stage label update in real time as the backend runs through 7 stages: scaffold prep → apo enzyme prep → pocket detection → fragment substitution → PDBQT conversion → AutoDock Vina docking → results analysis.
Processing time depends on how many substitution sites and what MW range you chose. A single site with 0–50 Da typically takes 2–5 minutes; three sites at 0–350 Da can take 20–40 minutes or even more, as thousands of fragments are docked.
Step 7 of 7
Download Your Results
When complete, two download buttons appear. The Results CSV lists the top 3 ranked ligands with their SMILES, molecular weight, and Vina binding affinities for each conformation. The PDBQT ZIP contains all docked ligand files plus the prepared receptor — load them directly into PyMOL or AutoDock for further analysis.
CSV gives best 3 ranked ligands, pertaining to the number of conformations selected for: Lower (more negative) affinity = stronger predicted binding. Open the ZIP files in PyMOL to observe load apo_enzyme.pdbqt along with load rank_001_ligand_1_docked.pdbqt to see the best docking arrangement.
